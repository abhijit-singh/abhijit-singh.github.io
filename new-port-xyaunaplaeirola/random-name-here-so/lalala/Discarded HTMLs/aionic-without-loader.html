While this is an constantly evolving project with regular iterations continuing as I write this case study, most core modules have already been built. What you’ll find below is the a bunch of highlights and problems solved over the course of it’s evolution.

Background & Research

With most of the mainstream medical software stuck in the 90s, you don’t have to go too far to find inefficiencies, inconsistencies and outright gaps in the systems being used in India’s leading diagnostic laboratories. With Sigtuple launching it’s chain of laboratories under the brand name ‘Humain Health’ (then called ‘Sigtuple Labs’) in 2017, very quickly it became apparent that no tool available in the market was apt enough to power the envisioned next generation of laboratories.

Understanding the Problems

While some shortfalls in the existing systems were attributed to the unconventional business objectives, we also identified major pain-points for the users that could no longer be overlooked. While the project came into existence with the core pain point that data collection in the desired format was proving to be increasingly tedious with any alternate software, we ended up discovering a bunch of user issues when we closely studied the process and stepped into the user’s shoes.

The project came into existence with the core pain point that data collection in the desired format was proving to be increasingly tedious.

Getting to Know the Users

Having the privilege of the end users being directly accessible, our first plan of action was to understand their exact process and embody their modus operandi. We set up ourselves in one of our labs for a couple of days and shadowed lab technicians, accession staff and pathologists. We then interviewed a couple of lab managers.

Lab technicians interacting with machnies processing blood samples in one of Sigtuple’s Labs.

The software being used at the time was the third one being tried, in a span of 2 months, and was believed to be better than anything else available in the marktet. Yet we were able to identify numerous gaps in the system, both operationally and in terms of things that could be automated and optimized in the softwate. We spent a couple of days brainstorming internally to figure out everything we could build to fill the gaps.

Competitor Analysis

Apart from the software already being used in the labs at the time, we studied a couple more diagnostic laboratory managment systems that were being used by leading laboratories.

We split into teams, did an exhaustive analysis of all available features, plus-points, gaps, and deal-breakers in each and collated our findings into a single report. At the end of this exercise we gathered the medical and business teams to illustrate our findings and present a tentative roadmap. This exercise took one full week and helped us align with the priorities of the business as well as the users, and filter out multiple superficial problems we were paying unwarranted attention towards.

Problems Identified

01 Data Extraction

While the diagnostic ecosystem recent times has significantly advanced in terms of machine capabilities and processing power, there is almost no consistency in the data produced by different machines. Most lab softwares were unable to interface with a majority of the diagnostic machines to extract formatted comprehensible data. Sigtuple was already using validated, anonymized data from multiple sources to power algorithms for it’s diagnostic machines, but data sanity had been a long-standing issue and procuring it was getting increasingly more expensive every passing day.

…when the aim was to make the system smart enough to minimize doctor intervention and manual error, getting a stable stream of accurate, comprehensible data was necessary.

02 Error Prone

With no system based checks on potentially critical data, the entire process was prone to human error - both clerical and computational.

03 Highly Inefficient

Made with a mindset where user experience was hardly a priority, in what seemed to be a ad-hoc manner. While the software gave put little restrictions on the user because of the generic nature of validations in place, basic everyday tasks were much more complicated than they should’ve been. Not only did this impact the business, but also user/employee satisfaction.

04 No Partner Collaboration

Because of the lack of an integrated system, the outsourcing and in-sourcing of samples to and from partner labs was forced to be a manual process. This made tracking, accountability and accuracy an ever challenging task as we scaled up.

04 Outdated Patient Experience

The options to provide supporting interfaces to patients for booking appointments, availing tests & consultations in laboratories, tracking progress and receiving reports were non-existent.

With a seeming vacuum for intuitive, feature rich lab-managment softwares in the market, we decided to build a system that could not only power our own laboratories, but a vanilla version of which could also be sold/provided to partner laboratries, in turn multiplying the sources of data available to us for R&D of our diagnostic models and devices.

Process

Modules

With the aim of distributing variations of the software to multiple labs with varying requirements, it was only obvious to keep it as modularized as possible, such that handpicked modules could be plugged together and provided as required.

Personas

We charted out the roles and responsibilities of different people in the lab during our visit and collated them under 6 different roles. Each role had a specific set of tasks which defined their key motivations and goals, a brief summary of which has been given below.

Receptionist/Helper

Register new customers, book appointments and oversee queue managment.

Phlebotomist

Collect blood, urine, semen samples from the customers.

Accessioning Staff

Log incoming samples into the system and transfer them to the respective department.

Lab Technicians

Receive samples for their department, process them using devices & log results.

Doctors

Oversee sample processing and validate results entered by lab technicians.

Customer

Book appointments, track samples and access reports.

Information Flow

One of the more complicated parts of the system was tracking the sample lifecycle with respect to each order placed by a customer or partner lab, and mapping the roles, responsibilities & restrictions for different lab staff.

Brainstorm, Sketch, Iterate, Review

Numerous brainstorming sessions, excel sheets and google docs later (that I’m unfortunately not at liberty to share), we finally got to creating sketching interfaces. We mapped out various user flows and sketched out barebones UI screens that could work. We constantly sought feedback from the medical team to make sure we’re not stuck in a bubble.

However, one key realization during this period was that sketches or wireframes are not too effective for taking feedback from non-technical users who usually find it difficult to look past the superficial elements and focus on the functionality.

Designs

Initial Screens

With a figurative gun on our head to launch Aionic as soon as possible, we hastily put together a bunch of screens for validation by the medical team. While now the product looks completely different, below are the intial set of wireframes we came up with.

Intial UI Screens of Aionic v0.0.1

Being a data driven company, we were quick to add basic data analytics on the platform and track usage of each feature, analyze interaction patterns and identify areas where improvement was most needed.

Instead of going through the entire journey of how we iterated, tested and reached to what Aionic is today, I’ll highlight a bunch of key challenges and their solutions that proved to be instrumental to the it’s success.

Key Solutions

The first phase of the project was fairly straight-forward and it was vital that we got the basic flows of the system correct. We focused on launching a quick and dirty version but ensured that it was functionally robust. Once we had that in place, we moved to solving more complex problems and focused optimized flows that impacted the top line.

The Almighty Worklist

The worklist is the list of samples currently in the laboratory for processing along with their history of processing and all actionables to be performed on them. We realized that this was the module that each type of user spent over 70% of their time interacting with. Given this data, it was clear that optimizing the interaction in the worklist was necessary for the system to be successful. Here’s what I came up with to facilitiate that:

Role Based Views

Each team in the lab had a specific task to perform in the sample lifecycle. For example, the phlebotomist is only concerned with collecting the sample and passing it on to the accession department, the accession staff needs to register all incoming samples and segrate them to be sent to different mediacl departments, and so on. I thus created an experience where each user would by default see a specific screen on navigating to the worklist module, which pertained to their specific role. Role based permissions and configuration would dictate if at all they would be able to access the interface pertaining to other teams.

Role based view where a ‘Phlebotomist’ by default sees the ‘To Be Collected’ with the Sample Status filter pre-applied

Smart Filters

I designed context based filters that would be applied by default once the user logs into the system. This helped reduce manual, repetitive interaction with filters. Filters are now pre-applied based on the medical department, the pending tasks for the day of the user and previous actions. Based on feedback from the engineering team, this was later extended to provide suggestive results when the user manually interacts with the filters.

Filters of Department, Date, Source & Client are pre-applied for the user’s default view based on their role and pending tasks.

Role Based Views and Smart Filters reduced the overall clicks in the worklist by over 63% and time needed to process each sample by at-least 15%, enabling the lab to reduce the promised turn-around-time required to process each sample, potentially saving lives.

Context, History & Warnings

For entering and validating results for any test, we designed the system such that lab technicians and doctors always have a context of the patient’s details that they might need, in order to correlate results. In addition to this, we added custom warnings based on reference ranges, historical values for the patient and demographic patterns.

Warnings based on reference range and historical values shown in the reports section while maintaining the context of the order and patient details.

Bulk Actions

As a lot of the time, the users, particularly in the accession departments need to take the same action on a bunch of samples which ranges from 2 to 20, I designed an interaction pattern that allowed the user to take that action in a single click, hence shaving off multiple clicks and context switches from the task.

Bulk actions appear in the secondary navigation bar as soon as the user selects multiple samples

Interfacing Machines

While the engineering team worked on building a template for interfacing every type of diagnostic machine available on the market, I designed an interface to inititiate, track and monitor the interfacing and information flow from each machine available in the lab with the server.

Device managment UI available on the master server in each lab

This interface reduced the dependence on the engineering team every time a new machine was to be installed, caliberated or re-mapped and cut short a process that used to take over a day into a few minutes.

Special report formats

A major roadblock in our pursuit to collect structured data came when we came across tests without a standard parameter-value format. Many times the device used to process a few tests gave the report in a complex format which were difficult to parse without a pre-defined structure. In other cases, the number of parameters was dynamic and inseparably-related to each other, hence making little sense when studied individually. We hence came up with a concept to define inter-parameter relationships and embed them into pre-defined templates that supported charts, graphs, images, and tables.

Test and Parameter Configurations with options for special report formats

Group Validation & Order Managment

There are cases where multiple tests ordered by a patient might be correlated to each other. In such a scenario, doctors earlier needed to open them separately and manually correlate based on memory. In an attempt to simplify this, I created a single view where results of associated tests could be viewed and validated at once.

Order details page with department-wise grouping

Pre-Barcoded Collection Tubes

A major problem in diagnostics is a mixup of samples, which can have critical consequences more often than not. We came up with the idea to pre-barcode our tubes even before sample collection, and distribute these tubes to all phlebotomists, collection centers and partners. This enabled us to track each tube and sample right from it’s collection to the final report generation. It also promoted accountability and helped us trace back any mismatch.

Design System

Along the way, as Aionic grew into a platform and associated products started propping up, I started compiling all components, UI patterns, micro-interactions, transitions and styles into a design library. After much convincing, I was finally able to onboard the engineering team to help me extend this into a proper design system.

Results & Reflection

We’ve come a long way since we started, both in this case study as well as in the product journey of Aionic. We can proudly say that very soon we’d achieved 100% of the targets we originally set out with, but those initial goals hardly seem daunting with what Aionic has now evolved to become.

Data Extraction

Aionic has been giving a stable stream of structured data to Sigtuple’s R&D team.

Not Error Prone

With system level checks, automation of routine tasks and an AI-based warning system, Aionic has helped Humain Health set a benchmark in accuracy.

Highly Efficient

Aionic has increased efficiency of the labs, increasing the processing capacity with the same amount of staff and machines. It has also helped Humain Health promise some of the lowest turn-around times in the industry.

Smooth Partner Collaboration

We’ve built an array of tools around Aionic facilitating Parnter labs and logistics.

Delightful Patient Experience

Custom notifications and a dedicated patient mobile app dedicated patient mobile app for Android and iOS, connected with Aionic, has helped Humain Health provide a delightful customer experience.

Aionic is now being used to process over 10,000 samples everyday in Humain Health labs & other partner labs accross India. Humain Health, powered by Aionic has also received the much valued NABL accreditation.

Learnings

This project has now continued for well over an year, undergone many iterations and has been a constant source of learnings.

01 User is king, of incremental improvements.

We had the privilege of being able to talk to the end users throughout this project, and while we got some wonderful insights and ideas from them, any major change to the existing workflow was almost always opposed at first. Many of those changes were correct to be put down, but many others are now in place and proving to be highly impactful. The end user may not always realize the effectiveness of a major change at first, but you have to remember that the user does not necessarily know what they need in the broader scheme of things.

02 Having a solid Design System pays off in the short run as well.

With a design system in place, we ended up eliminating wireframes from our process. Our speed of iteration and prototyping went up considerably, which in turn allowed us to test out multiple high fidelity solutions with the end user and get quick, actionable feedback.

03 It’s all about the constraints.

Sometimes you don’t have enough time to ship the solution you’d like, sometimes not enough data and sometimes not enough details. The conditions will never be ideal. What’s important is to make the most out of what you have, and put your best foot forward. And it’s okay to not strike gold in the first go, as long as you keep iterating, keep improving, keep innovating.
